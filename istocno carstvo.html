Eastern Roman Empire

The Eastern Roman Empire, known today as the Byzantine Empire, emerged in 330 AD when Emperor Constantine the Great established Constantinople as the new imperial capital. While the Western Roman Empire was in decline, the East flourished, becoming the beacon of Roman law, culture, and Christian faith.

At its height, the empire stretched from the Balkans and Greece through Asia Minor, including parts of the Levant and North Africa, acting as a bridge between Europe and Asia. Its fortified cities, grand churches, and majestic palaces showcased the sophistication and power of Byzantine civilization.

The empire was home to a diverse population: Greeks, Armenians, Slavs, Syrians, Egyptians, and Jews coexisted under a complex administrative and social system. Greek culture and language dominated intellectual and governmental life, while Christianity provided the moral and spiritual backbone of society.

Despite its strength, the empire faced continuous challenges: invasions by Persians, Arabs, and later Turks, internal political struggles, and economic pressures slowly eroded its power. The fall of Constantinople to the Ottoman Turks in 1453 marked the definitive end of the Eastern Roman Empire, but its legacy lived on in art, law, architecture, and the preservation of classical knowledge that would later fuel the Renaissance.
